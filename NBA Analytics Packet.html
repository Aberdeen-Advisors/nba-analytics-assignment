Welcome to the Challenge

You're an analytics consultant for a fictional NBA team. Your mission: dig through real player data, build a dashboard, and tell the General Manager which player to sign before the trade deadline. This is how consultants actually work.

The Situation

Imagine you're an analytics consultant hired by the Aberdeen Angels, an NBA team on the edge of title contention. The General Managers — Kavir Naik and David Wise — come to you and say:


— Kavir Naik & David Wise, GMs · Aberdeen Angels"We need to sign one more player before the trade deadline. We have $30 million to spend. I want someone who's been in the league 3 to 5 years — experienced enough to help us now, but not so expensive yet. Find me the best deal."


Your job: use the data to find that player and make a recommendation you can defend with numbers.

What You'll Learn

  • Excel: Finding and fixing messy data — a skill every analyst needs
  • Analytics thinking: How to choose the right stats and what they actually mean
  • Power BI: Building an interactive dashboard — the kind clients actually use
  • AI tools: Using AI to help draft professional write-ups
  • Presenting: Delivering a recommendation to executives who will push back on you

What You'll Produce

  • A cleaned dataset with every fix documented
  • A 3-tab Power BI dashboard the GM can actually use
  • An AI-assisted insights write-up
  • A 3-slide presentation to Aberdeen partners — with your recommendation

This goes on your resume. Build it like it matters.

📏

Ground Rules

Three rules that apply to every phase, every deliverable.

Rule 1 — Back It Up With Numbers

Any claim you make needs data behind it. "I think Player X is good" doesn't cut it. "Player X has the highest Win Shares per $10M among players who meet the GM criteria" does.

Rule 2 — Document Before You Fix

In Phase 3 (data cleaning), you must write down every problem you find in the Cleaning Log before you fix it. Real analysts do this so their work can be checked. If it's not in the log, it didn't happen.

Rule 3 — One Clear Answer

Your final presentation ends with one player. Not "it could be any of these three." One recommendation, backed by your data. Executives don't want options — they want a decision.

Understand the Goal

Figure out exactly what question you're trying to answer

▼

What's the point of this phase?

Before you open any data, you need to understand what you're actually trying to find. A good consultant always starts with the question, not the spreadsheet.

Read this first — your GM brief

FROM: Kavir Naik & David Wise, GMs — Aberdeen Angels

"Hey — we need to add one player before the trade deadline. We have $30 million in budget. I want someone who's been in the league for 3 to 5 years — they've got experience but they're not at their max contract yet, so the price is still reasonable. ✅

Example of a good paragraph: "My goal is to find the NBA player who gives the team the most wins per dollar spent, with a salary under $30 million and 3–5 years of league experience. To answer this, I'll need to look at each player's salary and how many wins they contribute to their team. My guess is that I'll find some lesser-known players on smaller teams who are actually better deals than the big stars."

04

Pick Your KPIs

Choose the stats that actually answer the GM's question

▼

What's a KPI?

KPI stands for "Key Performance Indicator." It's just a stat that directly answers your question. Not every stat in the dataset is useful — your job is to pick 3–4 that answer "who gives us the most wins per dollar?"

🚫

Don't use Points Per Game alone. A player who scores 30 points on terrible shooting actually hurts your team. A player who scores 12 points super efficiently might help you way more. Points per game without context is misleading.

Your tasks

  1. Read the KPI Guide tab in this packet. It explains 4 KPIs in plain language.
  2. Choose 3–4 KPIs for your analysis. Write each one down with: (a) what it measures, (b) why it helps answer the GM's question.
  3. Make sure one of your KPIs is "Bang for Buck" (Win Shares per $10M) — this is the most important one for comparing players across different salary levels.
  4. Open the KPI Calculator tab in the Excel template. It has the formula ready for you.

Deliverable

📝 List of your 3–4 KPIs with explanation — submitted as a Word doc

⚑ Gate 4 Question

"Why did you pick these KPIs? What would you be missing if you only looked at Points Per Game and salary?"

Find Your Top 3

What you're doing

The GM's filters (all three must apply)

  • Salary under $30 million
  • In years 3, 4, or 5 of their career (contract_year_number column)

Your tasks

KPI Guide

The 4 stats that actually answer "who gives us the most wins for our money?" — explained in plain English.

⚠️

Use these as a reference, not an answer key. Build your own KPI list first, then come here to check if your thinking is on track.

KPI 1 ⭐ Most Important
Bang for Buck (Win Shares per $10M)
For every $10 million we spend on this player, how many wins do they contribute? This directly answers the GM's question — who gives the most value for the money.
Formula: win_shares ÷ (salary_millions ÷ 10)
Good: above 1.5 | Great: above 2.5
KPI 2
Win Shares
How many of their team's wins is this player responsible for? Think of it like a score for the player's overall contribution to winning. A player with 8 win shares helped their team win 8 more games than they would have without them.
Column: win_shares
Good starter: 3+ | Star player: 7+
KPI 3
Availability (Games Played %)
Is this player actually healthy and playing? A great player who misses half the season doesn't help you. Divide games played by 82 to get their availability percentage.
Formula: games_played ÷ 82
Reliable: above 0.75 (62+ games)
KPI 4
Salary ($M)
Simply how much the player costs. You can't forget this one — it's in the GM brief. Must be under $30M for this analysis, but lower salary with the same production = better deal.
Column: salary_millions
GM limit: under $30M

Stats NOT to use as your main KPI

  • Raw Points Per Game alone: A player scoring 28 points on 10-for-30 shooting is actually hurting the team. Scoring volume without efficiency doesn't mean winning.
  • Field Goal % alone: A center who only dunks will have a high FG% but contributes less than a guard who takes harder shots. It doesn't account for the type of shots taken.
  • Total salary: $25M doesn't tell you anything on its own — is that expensive or cheap for what you're getting? Always compare salary to production.

The 4 Data Problems

There are exactly 4 problems hidden in NBA_2024_Raw.csv. Here are hints for each one. Use these after you've spent time looking yourself — don't jump straight here.

🚫

Try to find them yourself first. Use Excel filters and sorting to explore each column. If you're stuck after 15 minutes, come back here for a hint. The point is building the skill of spotting data problems — not just finding these specific ones.

🔢 Problem 1 — The FG% Format Is Wrong in Some Rows

Field goal percentage should always be a decimal between 0.30 and 0.70 (meaning 30% to 70%). But in some rows, someone stored it as a whole number instead — like 46.3 instead of 0.463.

💡 Hint: Sort the fg_pct column from highest to lowest. Any value bigger than 1.0 is definitely stored wrong. Count how many rows have this problem.
✅ Fixed when: All fg_pct values are between 0.30 and 0.70

💸 Problem 2 — Some Salaries Are Blank

About 10 players have no salary in the data. These are players on special low-salary contracts. You need to decide what to do with them — you can't just ignore them.

💡 Hint: Filter the salary_millions column and look for blank cells. In your Cleaning Log, write down how many blank salaries you found and what you decided to do (fill with $0, or exclude from salary analysis).
✅ Fixed when: No blank salary cells remain (fill with 0 or document exclusion)

👤 Problem 3 — Some Names Are Formatted Differently

Most players are listed as "First Last" (like "LeBron James"). But about 10 rows have it backwards: "Last, First" (like "James, LeBron"). This would break any sorting by name and looks messy.

💡 Hint: Sort the player_name column A to Z and scroll to the bottom — names with commas will cluster together. Fix them to "First Last" format.
✅ Fixed when: No player names contain a comma

🏀 Problem 4 — 3 Players Have More Starts Than Games Played

games_started should never be bigger than games_played. You can't start more games than you actually played in — that's logically impossible and means there's an error in the data.

💡 Hint: Add a new column in Excel with the formula =IF(games_started>games_played,"ERROR","OK") and filter for "ERROR." Fix by setting games_started equal to games_played for those rows.
✅ Fixed when: No rows where games_started is greater than games_played

Glossary

Quick definitions for terms you'll see in the data and in this project.

Win Shares
An estimate of how many team wins a player is responsible for. Higher = more valuable to the team.
Bang for Buck (WS/10M)
Win Shares divided by salary per $10M. The key metric for finding players who are great deals.
KPI
Key Performance Indicator — a specific metric that measures something important. Not just any stat.
Gate Check
A review at the end of each phase. You must pass it before moving forward.
Salary Cap
The maximum total amount an NBA team can spend on player salaries (about $136M in 2024).
Net Rating
A team's points scored minus points allowed per 100 possessions. The best single measure of team quality.
FG% (Field Goal %)
The percentage of shot attempts that go in. Stored as a decimal (0.46 = 46%). Should be between 0.30 and 0.70.
Contract Year Number
Which year of their NBA career the player is in. Years 3-5 = experienced but not yet max salary.
Hypothesis
Your guess about what you'll find before you look at the data. You write one in Phase 1.
Insights Memo
A short professional write-up that explains what you found and what it means. Not a summary — an argument.
Action Title
A slide title that tells the audience the finding, not just the topic. "Best values are in years 3–5" is an action title.
Trade Deadline
The specific date in the NBA season after which no player trades can happen. The GM needs a decision before this date.
